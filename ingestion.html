Data Ingestion System for Disaster Management

Our Data Ingestion System (DIS) helps collect, process, and analyze data from different sources to improve disaster response and preparedness. By gathering real-time and past data, it enables authorities to predict risks, send alerts, and make informed decisions to save lives and reduce damage.

How It Works

1. Collecting Data

DIS gathers information from different sources to get a full picture of a disaster:

  • Government Reports: Official updates, disaster impact assessments, and public records.
  • Satellites: Images of floods, wildfires, and weather patterns.
  • IoT Sensors: Devices that monitor water levels, temperature, and air quality in real time.
  • Social Media & Mobile Data: Analyzing posts and location-based updates for live situation reports.
  • Health Data: Reports on deaths and injuries to measure disaster impact.

2. Bringing Data into the System

Once collected, the data is sent to the system in two ways:

  • Batch Processing: Data is collected and processed at set times (e.g., government reports once a week).
  • Real-Time Streaming: Continuous data flow for live tracking (e.g., IoT sensors, social media).

3. Cleaning & Formatting Data

  • Removing Errors: Fixing missing or incorrect values.
  • Standardizing Formats: Converting different types of files into a common format.
  • Adding Tags: Timestamps and location details for better tracking.

4. Storing & Organizing Data

  • Data Lakes: Large storage for raw data.
  • Data Warehouses: Organized storage for analysis.

5. Analyzing & Making Predictions

  • Tracking Disasters Live: Monitoring social media, sensor updates, and weather changes.
  • AI & Machine Learning: Predicting flood levels, wildfire spread, and high-risk zones.

6. Sending Alerts & Helping Decision-Making

  • Disaster Alerts: SMS, app notifications, and chatbot warnings.
  • Flood & Fire Predictions: AI models predict disaster risks and suggest actions.
  • GIS Maps: Interactive maps show affected areas and evacuation routes.

7. Sharing Data & Communicating with the Public

The processed data is made accessible for decision-makers and citizens:

  • APIs & Reports: Agencies and NGOs get real-time disaster insights.
  • Public Dashboards: Websites and apps show disaster warnings and safety information.
  • Post-Disaster Analysis: Using satellite images and AI models to improve future predictions.

Why It Matters

  • Faster Response: Real-time alerts and live monitoring help in quick action.
  • Better Predictions: AI forecasts disaster severity, reducing loss of life and property.
  • Scalable & Secure: Can handle massive amounts of data while keeping it safe.
  • Smart Decision-Making: Helps governments, relief agencies, and communities prepare better.

Mortality Data (Deaths Reported)

Mortality data refers to the number of deaths reported during or after a disaster. It is collected from various sources, such as hospitals, emergency response teams, and local government authorities. This data provides crucial insights into the human impact of the disaster.

How Is the Data Collected?

  • Hospitals and Clinics: Medical facilities record the number of deaths caused by the disaster.
  • Death certificates issued by hospitals serve as an official record.
  • Emergency Centers and First Responders: Data is gathered by rescue teams working in affected areas.
  • Local Government Authorities: Municipal offices collect reports from multiple agencies.
  • Community Reports: In remote areas, local communities or NGOs may provide informal estimates.

Data Formats:

  • Reports: Plain text or PDF documents summarizing casualty numbers.
  • APIs: Machine-readable formats like JSON or XML.
  • Spreadsheets (CSV): Location-wise or day-wise mortality figures.

Uses of Mortality Data:

  • Severity Assessment: Mortality data serves as a direct indicator of disaster severity.
  • Example: A high number of reported deaths suggests either a severe disaster or ineffective preparedness.
  • Resource Allocation: Helps prioritize medical supplies and relief resources.
  • Example: Areas with the highest death toll during a flood receive the first deployment of aid.
  • Policy Improvements: Allows authorities to analyze gaps in preparedness.
  • Example: After analyzing heatwave mortality data, public cooling centers may be implemented.

Example Scenarios:

Earthquake Example:

  • Hospitals report 1,000+ casualties.
  • Emergency responders notice most deaths occurred in collapsed high-rise buildings.
  • Actionable Steps: Introduce stricter building codes and earthquake preparedness training.

Challenges in Mortality Data Collection:

  • Delayed Reporting: Some deaths may not be reported immediately, especially in remote areas.
  • Data Duplication: Multiple agencies might report the same deaths, inflating figures unless verified.
